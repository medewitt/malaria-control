# Diagnostic Protocols for Infectious Disease Screening

The following table summarizes the diagnostic approaches for malaria, tuberculosis, and other infectious diseases identified in the sources, including their effectiveness and typical turnaround times (TAT).

### **Malaria Diagnostic Approaches**

| Diagnostic Approach | Sensitivity | Specificity | Turnaround Time (TAT) | Key Characteristics & Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Microscopy (Gold Standard)** | ~71.4% (field estimate); [1] insensitive for low parasite densities. [2, 3] | ~47.3% (field estimate). [1] | Within a few hours. [4, 5] | Proficiency of the microscopist is critical; provides species and density information. [5, 6] |
| **Rapid Diagnostic Test (RDT)** | 92% (*P. falciparum*); 72.9% (*P. vivax*). [7] (97.0% in specific trial data). [8] | 96.6% (*P. falciparum*); 99.1% (*P. vivax*). [7] (96.8% in specific trial data). [8] | ~15 minutes. [9] | Simple, field-friendly; performance varies by brand, batch, and target antigen (e.g., HRP2 vs. LDH). [7, 9] |
| **Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)** | 98–100%. [10] | 88–94%. [10] | Not available quickly enough for acute clinical diagnosis. [11] | High-throughput; identifies species and drug-resistance genes; requires specialized equipment. [3, 10] |
| **Loop-Mediated Isothermal Amplification (LAMP)** | High; effective for low-density and subpatent infections. [3] | Not explicitly stated. | Faster than lab PCR; high throughput. [3, 12] | Field-applicable for species detection at low density with less equipment than standard PCR. [3, 13] |
| **Biosensors (Blue-ray device based)** | 98.1%. [14] | 54.8%. [14] | Not stated (implied automated). [14] | Promising for detecting asymptomatic individuals; still less commonly used. [14] |

### **Other Infectious Disease Diagnostics**

| Disease | Approach | Effectiveness | Turnaround Time (TAT) | Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Tuberculosis (TB)** | **Smear Microscopy** | 50–60% sensitive. [2, 15] | Not stated. | Traditional method; scan for acid-fast bacilli in sputum. [15] |
| **Tuberculosis (TB)** | **Xpert MTB/RIF** | Rapid and sensitive; identifies rifampicin resistance. [16] | < 2 hours. [16] | Cartridge-based DNA amplification; used for initial diagnosis in high-risk patients. [16] |
| **HIV** | **ELISA** | High sensitivity and specificity. [17] | 2–14 days. [17] | Detects antibodies in blood or saliva; requires Western blot confirmation. [17] |
| **HIV** | **Rapid HIV Test** | High sensitivity and specificity. [17] | ~20 minutes. [17] | Produced from a finger prick; a "preliminary positive" requires confirmation. [17] |
| **Syphilis** | **RPR Test** | Varies; requires serum separation and plate shaker. [18] | "Rapid." [18] | Nontreponemal test; reagents require refrigeration. [18] |
| **Syphilis** | **Dual POC (HIV/Syphilis)** | Sensitive and specific for active syphilis. [18] | "Rapid" (same-day). [18, 19] | Detects both treponemal and nontreponemal antibodies; uses finger prick. [18] |

**Note on Sepsis:** Research is ongoing to develop **point-of-care tests (POCTs) for sepsis** with performance targets of 83% sensitivity and 94% specificity to be considered cost-effective relative to current clinical assessment. [20, 21]
